## Abstract

Lactase (β-galactosidase, EC 3.2.1.23) represents a critical enzyme in human digestive physiology, catalyzing the hydrolysis of lactose into its constituent monosaccharides, glucose and galactose. Despite the widespread prevalence of lactase deficiency affecting approximately 65-70% of the global adult population, comprehensive understanding of the enzyme's biochemical mechanisms, regulatory pathways, and activity modulation remains an active area of investigation. This research paper presents a systematic examination of lactase enzyme activity, focusing on its catalytic properties, structural features, and functional significance in human nutrition and health.

The primary objective of this investigation is to establish a comprehensive framework for understanding lactase enzymatic activity through integrated analysis of its molecular structure, substrate specificity, kinetic parameters, and physiological regulation. The research employs a multi-faceted methodological approach combining literature review of established biochemical principles, analysis of enzyme kinetics data, examination of genetic and developmental regulatory mechanisms, and evaluation of clinical implications associated with lactase persistence and non-persistence phenotypes.

This study addresses several critical dimensions of lactase function: the molecular basis of catalytic activity including active site architecture and substrate binding mechanisms; the genetic and epigenetic factors governing lactase expression throughout human development; the evolutionary significance of lactase persistence polymorphisms in different populations; and the broader implications for dietary recommendations, nutritional interventions, and management of lactose intolerance.

The significance of this research extends beyond fundamental biochemistry to encompass practical applications in clinical nutrition, food science, pharmaceutical development, and personalized dietary planning. By synthesizing current knowledge of lactase biochemistry with emerging insights into genetic variation and physiological adaptation, this work contributes to the expanding field of nutritional genomics and evidence-based dietary guidance. The findings are expected to inform clinical practice, enhance understanding of enzyme regulation in digestive physiology, and provide a foundation for future research into therapeutic interventions for lactose malabsorption.
